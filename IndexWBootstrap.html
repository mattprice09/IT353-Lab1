Assignments

Mashups: The new breed of Web app is all about the new age of web applications - mashups. Essentially, a mashup is a web app that utilizes different data sources or APIs. As an example, I'll use my Home Fly project listed above. We utilized both the Google Places API and web-scraped data about shelter listings. These were conglomerated into a web app that matched the addresses from the web-scraped data to the actual location that Google provided.

One of the things in the article that stuck with me the most was about Ajax. First off, the article states that Ajax requires JavaScript to be enabled. Because of this, robots, spiders, and web crawlers are unable to navigate through your page if you use Ajax frequently. This is a huge piece of information as this is capable of destroying a web page's search engine visibility. Not only that, but the article explains that, if user-updated content is tied only to an Ajax call, it will not be reinstated when the BACK button is pressed. Perhaps this is what happens when you fill out a form, click Next, then go back, only to find that you have to redo the entire form.
The man who wants to translate the Web is an article about a guy's startup company. The company is called Duolingo and its purpose is to make the Web intelligible through any spoken language. That is, the company's goal is to essentially be able to translate any web page. Obviously, this would be a great accomplishment. There are just a couple of drawbacks.

First off, finding enough people to accurately translate between two languages is difficult. To add to that, PAYING these people who can translate for the company would be nearly impossible. This is how Duolingo evolved into its current business model: for the price of your education results, you can learn a language for free on their platform. The company simply uses your data as food for their machine learning algorithms and, ultimately, their database.
